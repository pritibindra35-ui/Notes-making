Literature Analysis Note Templates Study Strategies

Note-Making Fundamentals

  • Better Retention: Writing helps encode information in long-term memory
  • Quick Revision: Condensed notes save time during exam preparation
  • Understanding Connections: Notes help you see relationships between themes and ideas
  • Exam Confidence: Well-organized notes boost confidence and performance

Clarity

Use clear headings, bullet points, and structured layouts. Your future self should understand your notes easily.

Conciseness

Capture key ideas in your own words. Avoid copying entire paragraphs; summarize instead.

Connections

Link themes, characters, and literary devices. Show how different elements relate to each other.

Consistency

Develop a consistent format and stick to it. This makes reviewing and finding information easier.

Physical Tools
  • Quality notebooks (A4 size recommended)
  • Different colored pens (blue/black for text, red for important points)
  • Highlighters (3-4 different colors)
  • Ruler for neat diagrams and tables
  • Sticky notes for quick reminders
Digital Tools
  • Notion or OneNote for organized digital notes
  • Google Docs for collaborative study
  • Canva for visual mind maps
  • Grammarly for writing assistance
  • PDF annotators for textbook notes

Literary Device Analysis

Metaphor & Simile

Note Format: "Device: [Type] | Quote: [Text] | Effect: [Impact on meaning]"

Example: Device: Metaphor | Quote: "Life's but a walking shadow" | Effect: Emphasizes life's brevity and meaninglessness
Imagery

Note Format: "Type: [Visual/Auditory/etc.] | Purpose: [Why used] | Effect: [Reader's response]"

Example: Type: Visual | Purpose: Create atmosphere | Effect: Builds tension and foreboding
Symbolism

Note Format: "Symbol: [Object/Image] | Represents: [Abstract concept] | Context: [When/where used]"

Example: Symbol: Green light | Represents: Hope and the American Dream | Context: End of Daisy's dock

Character Analysis Techniques

Character Profile Method
  • Physical Description: Key appearance details
  • Personality Traits: 3-5 main characteristics
  • Motivations: What drives the character
  • Relationships: How they interact with others
  • Development: How they change throughout the story
  • Quotes: Key dialogue that reveals character
Character Development Tracking

Create a timeline showing how characters evolve:

Beginning → Conflict → Crisis → Resolution
Note personality changes, new insights, or shifts in relationships at each stage.

Theme Identification Strategies

Question Method

Ask yourself:

  • What is the author trying to say about life?
  • What patterns do I notice?
  • What conflicts are presented?
  • How do characters learn or grow?
Repetition Tracking

Look for:

  • Repeated words or phrases
  • Recurring symbols or images
  • Similar situations or conflicts
  • Parallel character experiences
Connection Mapping

Create links between:

  • Characters and their actions
  • Events and their consequences
  • Symbols and their meanings
  • Conflicts and resolutions

Poetry Example: Shakespeare's Sonnet 18
Sample Notes

Title: "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"
Poet: William Shakespeare

Form & Structure:
• Type: Shakespearean sonnet
• Rhyme scheme: ABAB CDCD EFEF GG
• Meter: Iambic pentameter
• Structure: 3 quatrains + 1 couplet

Theme: Eternal beauty through poetry

Key Literary Devices:
• Metaphor: Comparing beloved to summer's day
• Personification: "rough winds do shake" (line 3)
• Metaphor: "eye of heaven" for sun (line 5)
• Paradox: Eternal life through mortal verse

Analysis:
Lines 1-8: Summer is beautiful but imperfect
Lines 9-12: Beloved's beauty is eternal
Lines 13-14: Poetry grants immortality

Key Quote:
"So long as men can breathe or eyes can see, / So long lives this, and this gives life to thee"
Significance: Poetry immortalizes beauty beyond physical death

Prose Example: "The Luncheon" by W. Somerset Maugham
Sample Notes

Title: "The Luncheon"
Author: W. Somerset Maugham

Setting:
• Time: 20 years after the incident, flashback to past
• Place: Foyot's restaurant in Paris
• Atmosphere: Elegant, expensive

Characters:
• Narrator: Young, struggling writer (broke)
• Woman: Middle-aged admirer (manipulative, pretentious)

Plot Summary:
• Young writer meets woman admirer
• She suggests expensive lunch
• Orders costly items despite his budget
• He can only afford coffee
• Years later: poetic justice served

Themes:
• Exploitation of kindness
• Social pretension
• Poetic justice

Literary Techniques:
• Irony: Woman becomes very fat (justice)
• Humor: Self-deprecating narrator
• Contrast: Past struggle vs. present success

Key Quote:
"I have had my revenge at last"
Significance: Time brings justice for past wrongs

Note-Making in Action: Step-by-Step Process
Step 1: First Reading

Read for general understanding. Don't take notes yet. Focus on the overall story, mood, and main ideas.

Step 2: Active Reading

Second reading with highlighter. Mark key quotes, literary devices, and important character moments.

Step 3: Note Creation

Use your template to organize findings. Write in your own words, make connections, analyze significance.

Step 4: Review & Refine

Review notes for clarity and completeness. Add connections between themes and characters.

Effective Revision Strategies

Revision Timeline
  • Day 1: Take detailed notes
  • Day 3: Quick review (10 minutes)
  • Day 7: Comprehensive review (20 minutes)
  • Day 21: Test yourself (30 minutes)
  • Day 50: Final review before exam

  • Strengthens neural pathways
  • Improves long-term retention
  • Builds exam confidence

  • Write themes from memory
  • Explain concepts aloud
Study Groups
  • Discuss themes together

  • Practice essay questions
Teaching Method

  • Create presentations
  • Make video summaries

  • Review all major themes
  • Memorize key quotes
  • Practice essay outlines
  • Identify knowledge gaps
  • Create quick reference sheets
Day Before Exam
  • Light review only
  • Read key quotes once
  • Get proper sleep
  • Prepare exam materials
  • Stay calm and confident
